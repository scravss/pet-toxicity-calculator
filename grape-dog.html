My Dog Ate Grapes or Raisins —
Is It Dangerous?

Grapes and raisins are among the most unpredictably dangerous foods for dogs. They can trigger acute kidney failure with no established safe dose — even a single grape has caused fatal outcomes in some dogs.

Why Are Grapes and Raisins Dangerous for Dogs?

Despite decades of research, the exact toxic compound in grapes and raisins that causes kidney failure in dogs has not yet been definitively identified. This makes them uniquely dangerous — we cannot predict a safe minimum dose. Recent veterinary research points to tartaric acid as a likely culprit, but the mystery remains partially unsolved.

What is well established is the outcome: grape and raisin ingestion can cause acute renal failure (ARF) in dogs, often within 24–72 hours. The kidneys stop filtering waste from the blood, which rapidly becomes life-threatening without emergency intervention.

Key facts that make this toxin especially alarming:

  • There is no established safe dose — any amount must be treated as an emergency
  • Raisins are more dangerous by weight than fresh grapes (concentrated form)
  • Grape juice, wine, and products containing raisins (trail mix, cookies) are equally toxic
  • Some dogs survive large doses; others develop fatal kidney failure from a small amount — individual sensitivity varies widely
🚨 Critical: There is no safe amount of grapes or raisins for dogs. Any ingestion — even a single grape — warrants immediate veterinary contact. Do not wait for symptoms.

What Are the Symptoms of Grape or Raisin Toxicity?

Symptoms typically begin within 6 to 12 hours of ingestion and progress rapidly as kidney damage develops. Watch for:

  • Vomiting and/or diarrhea — often appearing within hours, sometimes containing grape/raisin pieces
  • Lethargy, weakness, unusual stillness
  • Loss of appetite (anorexia)
  • Abdominal pain or sensitivity when touched near the abdomen
  • Excessive thirst and urination (early kidney stress)
  • Decreased or absent urination (a serious sign of kidney failure)
  • Tremors, shaking
  • Seizures and coma (severe, late-stage toxicity)
⚠️ Decreased urination (oliguria) or complete cessation (anuria) after grape/raisin ingestion is a medical emergency. The kidneys may be failing. Go to the vet immediately.

Immediate Emergency Steps to Take

  1. Act immediately — do not wait for symptoms. This is the single most important rule with grape toxicity. The absence of symptoms does not mean safety; kidney damage can begin silently.
  2. Identify and record what was consumed. Fresh grapes, raisins, sultanas, currants, or a product containing them? Estimate the quantity as precisely as possible.
  3. Call your vet or a poison helpline right now. US: ASPCA +1-888-426-4435 | UK: Animal PoisonLine 01202 509000. Describe the amount and timing clearly.
  4. Inducing vomiting may be recommended by your vet if ingestion was very recent (within 1–2 hours) and your dog is alert. Do not do this without explicit veterinary instruction.
  5. Use our calculator to document the risk level and share it with your vet to support triage and treatment decisions.
  6. Follow up with blood tests. Even if your dog seems fine, your vet may recommend kidney function tests (BUN, creatinine) 24–48 hours after ingestion to detect early renal damage.
